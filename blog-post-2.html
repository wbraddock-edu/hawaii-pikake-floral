Lei Etiquette and History in Hawaii

Hawaiian Culture · May 22, 2025 · 5 min read

The lei is perhaps Hawaii's most recognizable cultural symbol — a garland of flowers, leaves, shells, feathers, or other natural materials given as a sign of love, respect, celebration, and aloha. But for visitors and newcomers to Hawaii, understanding the proper etiquette around leis can feel mysterious. Here's everything you need to know.

The History of the Lei

Leis predate Western contact in Hawaii by centuries. Ancient Hawaiians used leis in religious ceremonies, to honor royalty (ali'i), and to mark significant life events. The most prestigious ancient leis were made of feathers (hulu) and were worn exclusively by Hawaiian royalty. The mamo and o'o birds, whose feathers were used in these royal leis, are now extinct.

After Western contact in 1778, the lei tradition expanded and evolved. Pacific Islander immigrants — particularly from the Philippines, Japan, and Portugal — brought their own flower-stringing traditions that merged with Hawaiian practices. By the early 20th century, giving leis to arriving visitors had become a beloved island tradition, particularly at the Honolulu harbor when steamships arrived from the mainland.

Types of Lei

There are dozens of traditional lei styles, each with its own technique. The most common include: haku (braided, often worn as a head lei), kui (needle-strung, like pikake or plumeria), wili (wound with ferns and flowers), and hip'u (knotted style). Each style suits different occasions and flowers.

Proper Lei Etiquette

When receiving a lei, always accept it graciously — refusing a lei is considered rude. A lei should be placed around the neck, never removed in front of the giver. If you must remove a lei (due to allergies, for example), do so privately and always thank the giver warmly.

A closed lei (with both ends joined) should never be given to a pregnant woman, as it symbolizes something encircling the baby. Instead, an open-ended lei is appropriate for expectant mothers. This is an important detail many visitors don't know.

Leis should be disposed of respectfully. The traditional Hawaiian practice is to return them to nature — scatter the flowers in the ocean or in a natural setting. Never throw a lei in the trash while others are watching.

Lei Day in Hawaii

May 1st is Lei Day in Hawaii — a statewide celebration of the lei and Hawaiian culture. Pikake Floral Studio participates in the annual Lei Day festival at Kapiolani Park, where we offer lei-making demonstrations and special arrangements. It's one of our favorite days of the year.
